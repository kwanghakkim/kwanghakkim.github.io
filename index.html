A car can't move sideways — yet to parallel-park, sideways is exactly where it has to end up. It can only get there through a careful sequence of the moves it can make, and finding a single feedback rule that reliably parks it from any starting position has occupied control theorists for decades. That is the kind of problem I work on, and I am now chasing the harder version of it in 3D.

I am a PhD candidate in Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering at UC San Diego (UCSD), advised by Prof. Miroslav Krstić and Prof. Mamadou Diagne. My research is in nonlinear and safety-critical control of underactuated, nonholonomic vehicles — systems such as cars, unicycles, and their aerial and marine counterparts that cannot move freely in every direction. Using control Lyapunov and barrier function (CLF/CBF) methods, I design feedback laws that stabilize and keep these vehicles safe under tight limits on state, actuation, and uncertainty. Concretely, I work on nonholonomic vehicle parking, robust safety filters for unknown moving obstacles, and high-relative-degree safety constraints, building designs that are at once safe, inverse-optimal, adaptive, and able to converge in fixed or prescribed time. Most recently, I have been extending this work into three dimensions, toward autonomous underwater and aerial vehicles (AUVs and UAVs).

I hold an M.S. in Mechanical Engineering from UC San Diego and a B.S. in Aerospace Engineering from The University of Texas at Austin. Before my doctoral studies, I worked on autonomous systems as an undergraduate researcher under Prof. Ufuk Topcu.

Away from research, I golf and dig into new video games. My favorite course in San Diego is the Steel Canyon Golf Club, and my game of the year for 2025 is Clair Obscur: Expedition 33!
